ISR Cap. 7
In Force

Interpretation Act

  1. Short title and commencement
    1. This Act may be cited as the "Interpretation Act".
    2. The following provisions of this Act are effective as of 7 November 2021.
  2. Written law
    1. Every Act of Estates is considered to be a public Act and shall be judicially noticed as such unless the contrary is expressly provided by the Act.
    2. For written law to take effect as an Act of State, the document shall be signed and sealed by the Emperor upon promulgation.
    3. Every Schedule appended to an Act shall, together with any note thereto, be construed and have effect as part of the Act.
    4. The time of commencement of any Act shall be at the stroke of midnight on the stipulated effective date of provisions of the Act.
  3. Intrepretation of written law
    1. In the interpretation of a provision of a written law, an interpretation that would promote the purpose or object underlying the written law (whether that purpose or object is expressly stated in the written law or not) shall be preferred to an interpretation that would not promote that purpose or object. In the interpretation of a provision of a written law, if any material not forming part of the written law is capable of assisting in the ascertainment of the meaning of the provision, consideration may be given to that material.
  4. Consideration of extrinsic material
    1. Consideration to such material must be to confirm that the meaning of the provision is the ordinary meaning conveyed by the text of the provision taking into account its context in the written law and the purpose or object underlying the written law; or
    2. Consideration to such material must be to ascertain the meaning of the provision when —
      1. the provision is ambiguous or obscure; or
      2. the ordinary meaning conveyed by the text of the provision taking into account its context in the written law and the purpose or object underlying the written law leads to a result that is manifestly absurd or unreasonable.
    3. In determining whether consideration should be given to any material, or in determining the weight to be given to any such material, regard shall be had, in addition to any other relevant matters, to —
      1. the desirability of persons being able to rely on the ordinary meaning conveyed by the text of the provision taking into account its context in the written law and the purpose or object underlying the written law; and
      2. the need to avoid prolonging legal or other proceedings without compensating advantage.
    4. Consideration need not be given to material from other jurisdictions but may be inferred for reference or illustration purposes.
  5. Expiration of written law
    1. All written laws that have expired shall be judicially removed from the Public Registers. All written law shall be considered to have expired when either —
      1. the stated date of expiry on the Act or any written law, if any, has been reached; or
      2. the Act or written law has been repealed by subsequent written law.
  6. Subsidiary legislation
    1. Where appropriate, and for the purposes of specifically defining and refining the legislative intentions and provisions of any Act, the relevant authorities empowered and established by the Act shall set forth subsidiary legislation.
    2. Subsidiary legislation shall not contradict nor contravene any other written law in force at the time of formation, nor shall it contradict or contravene any written law that may be established after the formation of any subsidiary legislation.
    3. Subsidiary legislation, upon the repeal or expiration of the parent Act in part or in full, shall also be repealed or expire accordingly with respect to the parent Act.
  7. Powers and appointments
    1. Where a written law confers a power or imposes a duty on any natural person or the holder of an office, then, unless the contrary intention appears, the power may be exercised and the duty shall be performed from time to time as occasion requires, or by the holder of the office for the time being or by a person duly appointed to act for him.
    2. Where a written law confers upon any person or authority a power to make appointments to any office or place, the power shall, unless the contrary intention appears, be construed as including a power to dismiss or suspend any person appointed and to appoint another person temporarily in the place of any person so suspended or in place of any sick or absent holder of the office or place.
    3. Where the power of the person or authority to make appointments is only exercisable upon the recommendation or subject to the approval or consent of some other person or authority, the power of dismissal shall, unless the contrary intention appears, only be exercisable upon the recommendation or subject to the approval or consent of such other person or authority.
  8. Penal provisions
    1. The imposition of a penalty by any written law, in the absence of express provision to the contrary, shall not relieve any person from liability to answer for damages to a person injured.
    2. Where any act or omission constitutes an offence under 2 or more written laws, the offender shall, unless the contrary intention appears, be liable to be prosecuted and punished under any one of those written laws but shall not be liable to be punished twice for the same offence.
    3. A provision which constitutes an offence shall, unless the contrary intention appears, be deemed to provide also that an attempt to commit such offence shall be an offence against such provision, punishable as if the offence itself had been committed.
  9. Death
    1. A person shall be considered to have died when there has occurred either —
      1. irreversible cessation of circulation of blood and respiration in the body of the person; or
      2. total and irreversible cessation of all functions of the brain of the person, alternatively referred to as “brain dead”. In such circumstances where the person may be declared as such, there shall be 2 medical practitioners present at the point of certification.
  10. Capacity
    1. A person shall not be considered to be of capacity if —
      1. the person has yet to reach the age of 18;
      2. the person has been certified by a qualified medical practitioner appointed by the State to be lacking in capacity as a result of a physiological or psychological condition;
      3. the person has been involuntarily intoxicated or influenced by alcohol, chemical substances or drugs; or
      4. the person has an existing legal or contractual obligation in which the person may not be able to exercise capacity as would be possible under normal circumstances.
    2. No act committed whilst lacking in capacity pursuant to parts (b) and (c) of paragraph (1) of this section shall be deemed an offence.
    3. No agreement entered into whilst lacking in capacity pursuant to paragraph (1) of this section shall be deemed an enforceable contract.
